# The Agentic Oversight Framework


## The Benefits of AI Agents for Compliance

1. When deployed through the Agentic Oversight Framework, AI agents can make compliance officers an order of magnitude more effective at fighting financial crime.
2. The AOF enhances existing compliance frameworks by adding a layer that combines human oversight, real-time data, defined automated resolution pathways, and a comprehensive audit trail

## How does an AI Agent differ from other “Models”?

1. Key features of agentic AI include: goal-oriented behavior, autonomy, planing and decision-making, environment interaction, and persistence.
2. Comparing agentic AI with three familiar technologies commonly used in financial institutions:
 - **Rules-based systems** are great option for encoding policies using IF-THEN-ELSE logic, but are hard to scale and maintain as environments change
 - **ML systems** excel at identifying historical patterns and pattern matching, as well as prediction tasks. But ML systems cannot independently adapt their behavior when faced with new or evolving inputs.
 - **Workflow automation** can orchestrate multiple rules and ML models to automate decisioning, but are static in nature and require manual updates when business logic, user behavior, or data conditions change.

## The Compliance Challenge

1. In the United States, regulators including the OCC, Fed, FDIC, and FinCEN expect AI/ML models used in compliance functions to **follow SR 11-7-style governance**, validation, documentation, independent challenge, ongoing monitoring, explainability, audit trails, and clear answers about transparency, training data, bias, updates, and limitations.

2. BSA/AML (Bank Secrecy Act / Anti-Money Laundering) compliance remains **resource-intensive and operationally complex** because high false-positive rates, slow KYC processes, staff burnout, hiring difficulty, and outdated tools make traditional headcount-based scaling unsustainable, while AI agents can enhance rather than replace existing controls.

## The Agentic Oversight Framework (AOF) in-depth

1. Defined **Automated Resolution Pathways (ARP)** translate an institution’s SOPs, policies, procedures, and annotated historical reviews into specific AI-agent tasks with clear guardrails, success criteria, prompts, data sources, backtesting, shadow-mode validation, and human-reviewed recommendations.

2. Effective AI-agent review requires structured, current, validated, enriched, and clearly mapped r**isk intelligence data**, including KYC (Know Your Customer) documents, sanctions and PEP (Politically Exposed Person) alerts, adverse media, transaction monitoring results, device behavior, and third-party sources.

3. After processing a case through its ARP, the AI agent generates an approve, decline, or escalate recommendation with concise rationale, supporting-data links, and a decision-path summary, while the human compliance officer retains full authority over the final decision.

4. Under the AOF, every AI-agent action must be **traceable** through logs of screen and data interactions, consulted rules-based or machine-learning models, full recommendation rationale, and human reviewer accountability with timestamps and comments.

5. The AOF **integrates into the existing Group Risk and Control framework** by assigning first-line business units responsibility for day-to-day agent operation, AI operations/IT/data teams responsibility for monitoring and infrastructure, and executive and board committees responsibility for model governance, risk appetite alignment, and performance review.

6. The AOF provides **explainability** through feature attribution, decision tracing, counterfactual analysis, and human oversight integration, combining classic data science model evaluation with compliance best practices.

7. The AOF allows transaction monitoring agents to **operate at machine speed** by using structured inputs such as KYC documentation, sanctions and adverse media results, transaction history, risk signals, and device metadata to approve legitimate activity, escalate suspicious activity, compile evidence, and maintain a full audit trail.

8. The AOF creates a **virtuous cycle** in which every automated or human-made decision feeds back into the learning engine so agents become more accurate over time and new fraud-pattern intelligence can benefit participants almost immediately.

## Best Practices for AI Agent Adoption in Regulated Financial Services


1. **Secure integration within an approved compliance platform** is preferred over giving agents direct access to systems, laptops, or third-party tools because it limits risk and ensures comprehensive audit trails, traceability, monitoring, management, and observability.

2. AI agents can **improve first-line alert-review efficiency and strengthen second-line review** by consistently checking compliance-officer decisions, reducing mistakes caused by lapse in judgment, tiredness, or numbness from repetitive work.

3. Compliance teams should **begin with copilot mode**, where the agent conducts foundational research and prepares recommendations while humans own final decisions, before gradually moving toward auto-decisioning for lower-risk alerts supported by model validation and sampled human review.

## Use Cases and Case Studies of the AOF in Action

1. For step-up KYC, AI agents **help verify customer identity** by interpreting mismatches, cultural name variations, date formats, non-Latin alphabets, government-issued IDs, selfies, and liveness checks, producing faster onboarding and strong precision under a four-eyes human-review methodology.

2. For sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media alerts, AI agents trained on compliance SOPs compare names, addresses, birthdates, age, translations, state and address matches, and supplementary evidence, then **present Accept or Decline recommendations** for human compliance officers to determine final disposition.

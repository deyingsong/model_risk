# The Agentic Oversight Framework

## The Compliance Challenge


**4.1 The Regulatory Requirements for AI and Model Explainability**
In the United States, regulators including the OCC, Fed, FDIC, and FinCEN expect AI/ML models used in compliance functions to follow SR 11-7-style governance, validation, documentation, independent challenge, ongoing monitoring, explainability, audit trails, and clear answers about transparency, training data, bias, updates, and limitations.

**4.2 The Challenge of BSA/AML Compliance**
BSA/AML compliance remains resource-intensive and operationally complex because high false-positive rates, slow KYC processes, staff burnout, hiring difficulty, and outdated tools make traditional headcount-based scaling unsustainable, while AI agents can enhance rather than replace existing controls.

**5.1 Build Defined Automated Resolution Pathways (ARPs)**
Defined Automated Resolution Pathways translate an institution’s SOPs, policies, procedures, and annotated historical reviews into specific AI-agent tasks with clear guardrails, success criteria, prompts, data sources, backtesting, shadow-mode validation, and human-reviewed recommendations.

**5.2 Collect and Prepare Risk Intelligence Data**
Effective AI-agent review requires structured, current, validated, enriched, and clearly mapped risk intelligence data, including KYC documents, sanctions and PEP alerts, adverse media, transaction monitoring results, device behavior, and third-party sources.

**5.3 Decision and Present Findings from Automated Resolution Pathway**
After processing a case through its ARP, the AI agent generates an approve, decline, or escalate recommendation with concise rationale, supporting-data links, and a decision-path summary, while the human compliance officer retains full authority over the final decision.

**5.4 A Comprehensive Audit Trail**
Under the AOF, every AI-agent action must be traceable through logs of screen and data interactions, consulted rules-based or machine-learning models, full recommendation rationale, and human reviewer accountability with timestamps and comments.

**5.5 Governance Structure**
The AOF integrates into the existing Group Risk and Control framework by assigning first-line business units responsibility for day-to-day agent operation, AI operations/IT/data teams responsibility for monitoring and infrastructure, and executive and board committees responsibility for model governance, risk appetite alignment, and performance review.

**5.6 AI Explainability Framework**
The AOF provides explainability through feature attribution, decision tracing, counterfactual analysis, and human oversight integration, combining classic data science model evaluation with compliance best practices.

**5.7 Benefitting from Machine Speed**
The AOF allows transaction monitoring agents to operate at machine speed by using structured inputs such as KYC documentation, sanctions and adverse media results, transaction history, risk signals, and device metadata to approve legitimate activity, escalate suspicious activity, compile evidence, and maintain a full audit trail.

**5.8 Continuous Improvement**
The AOF creates a virtuous cycle in which every automated or human-made decision feeds back into the learning engine so agents become more accurate over time and new fraud-pattern intelligence can benefit participants almost immediately.

**6.1 Secure integration is better than “over the top” integration**
Secure integration within an approved compliance platform is preferred over giving agents direct access to systems, laptops, or third-party tools because it limits risk and ensures comprehensive audit trails, traceability, monitoring, management, and observability.

**6.2 Use agentic AI in the first and second-line for maximum benefit**
AI agents can improve first-line alert-review efficiency and strengthen second-line review by consistently checking compliance-officer decisions, reducing mistakes caused by lapse in judgment, tiredness, or numbness from repetitive work.

**6.3 Start with Copilot before moving to Auto Decisioning**
Compliance teams should begin with copilot mode, where the agent conducts foundational research and prepares recommendations while humans own final decisions, before gradually moving toward auto-decisioning for lower-risk alerts supported by model validation and sampled human review.

**7.1 Step-Up KYC Alert Decision Pathway Example and Case Study**
For step-up KYC, AI agents help verify customer identity by interpreting mismatches, cultural name variations, date formats, non-Latin alphabets, government-issued IDs, selfies, and liveness checks, producing faster onboarding and strong precision under a four-eyes human-review methodology.

**7.2 Sanctions / PEPs Alerts Agent Decision Pathways**
For sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media alerts, AI agents trained on compliance SOPs compare names, addresses, birthdates, age, translations, state and address matches, and supplementary evidence, then present Accept or Decline recommendations for human compliance officers to determine final disposition.

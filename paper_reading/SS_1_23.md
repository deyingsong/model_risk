# Model Risk Management Principles for Banks Supervisory Statement (Bank of England PRA)

## Background and definitions

- ‘Firms’ means UK banks, building societies and PRA-designated investment firms with internal model approval.
- A model is a quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process input data into output. The definition of a model includes input data that are quantitative and / or qualitative in nature or expert judgement-based, and output that are quantitative or qualitative.
- Model risk is the potential for adverse consequences from model errors or the inappropriate use of modelled outputs to inform business decisions.
- Model lifecycle includes (1) core modeling process, (2) model validation, and (3) model risk control.
- Primary roles related to models in organizations include model owner, model user, and model developer.


## Model risk management principles for banks

### Model identification and model risk classification

1. A model is **a quantitative method, system, or approach** that applies statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process input data into output.
2. A comprehensive **model inventory** should be maintained to enable firms to: identify the **sources of model risk**; provide the **management information** ((a) purpose and use of a model, (b) model assumptions and limitations, (c) findings from validation, (d) governance details) needed for reporting model risk; and help to **identify model inter-dependencies**.
3. Firms should implement a **consistent, firm-wide model tiering approach** that assigns a risk-based **materiality** (consider both (a) quantitative size-based measures and (b) qualitative factors) and **complexity rating** (should consider the risk factors that (a) impact a model’s inherent risk, (b) the use of alternative and unstructured data, and (c) measures of a model's interpretability, explainability, transparency, and the potential for designer or data bias to be present) to each of their models.

### Governance

1. The board should (1a) establish a comprehensive firm-wide MRM framework, (1b) set a model risk appetite, (1c) receive regular reports on the model risk profile, and (1d) provide challenge to the outputs, capabilities, limitations, operating boundaries, and mitigants of the most material models.
2. Firms should **identify the relevant SMF(s)** to assume overall responsibility for the MRM framework, its implementation, execution, and maintenance, while model owners, developers, and users **remain responsible for individual models**.
3. Firms should have **clearly documented, board-approved, and regularly reviewed policies and procedures** that formalise the MRM framework and cover **all aspects of the model lifecycle**, including model definition, tiering, development, data quality, validation, performance monitoring, mitigants, approval, and change.
4. Firms should clearly document roles and responsibilities for each stage of the model lifecycle and allocate them to staff with appropriate skills, experience, expertise, organisational independence, and seniority.
5. Internal Audit should periodically assess the effectiveness of the MRM framework over each component of the model lifecycle, verify compliance with internal policies and PRA expectations, and report findings to the board and relevant committees on a timely basis.
6. Boards and senior management remain **ultimately responsible** for model risk when using outsourcing, third-party vendor models, or parent-group models, and firms should validate such models or uses to the same standards as their internal MRM expectations.


### Model development, implementation, and use

1. All models should have a **clear statement of purpose and design objectives**, with conceptually sound modelling techniques, suitable design for intended use, reasonable and valid assumptions, and clear communication of merits, limitations, and sensitivities.
2. The model development process should demonstrate that **data** are suitable, representative, consistent with the chosen theory and methodology, free from inappropriate bias, compliant with relevant regulations, and that any adjustments, proxies, or alternative and unstructured data are documented, validated, monitored, and reflected in model tiering.
3. Model development testing should demonstrate that a **model works as intended** by assessing performance against design objectives through *backward-looking, forward-looking, sensitivity, challenger-model, benchmark, and material-change testing where appropriate*.
4. Firms should demonstrate that risks relating to model limitations and model uncertainties are **understood, monitored, and managed**, including through justified, documented, governed, and independently validated model adjustments or expert judgement.
5. Firms should maintain **comprehensive and up-to-date documentation** on model design, theory, logic, data, methodology, performance testing, limitations, and expert judgement so that an independent third party with relevant expertise can understand, assess, and replicate the model.
6. Models should be implemented in **thoroughly tested information systems or environments** subject to rigorous quality control and change control processes, with periodic reassessment of system suitability for the model purposes.

### Independent model validation

1. Firms should have an **independent validation function** with sufficient organisational standing to provide objective, unbiased, and critical opinions on model suitability, soundness, supporting systems, development data, outputs, reports, and model approvals.
2. All models should be subject to an **independent review**, with scope determined by model tier and model-change materiality, to assess model components, conceptual soundness, development evidence, qualitative judgements, and additional testing needed to identify and address limitations.
3. Firms should verify that model processes and systems implementation operate effectively and as intended, including model inputs, calculations, systems implementation, integration, user-developed applications, and reporting outputs.
4. Firms should conduct **ongoing model performance monitoring** against acceptable performance thresholds to ensure parameter estimates, model constructs, assumptions, usage, and responses to changing conditions remain appropriate and valid.
5. Firms should undertake **regular independent revalidation**, with frequency consistent with model tier, to determine whether the model has operated as intended and whether previous validation findings remain valid or require update, repetition, or augmentation.

### Model risk mitigants

1. Firms should have a consistent firm-wide process for applying **post-model adjustments** to address model limitations, supported by governance, documentation, senior management approval where material, transparent reporting, independent review, and root cause analysis.
2. Firms should consider **restricting model use** when significant deficiencies, errors, or performance breaches are identified, and should document, report, and track the nature of issues and remediation status in the model inventory.
3. For material models, firms should define **allowable exceptions** for model use and performance, implement **formally approved escalation procedures**, apply appropriate controls and mitigants, and promptly inform key stakeholders and senior management of model exceptions.

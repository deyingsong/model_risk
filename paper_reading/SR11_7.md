# Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management (SR Letter 11-7)

## Purpose and Scope

The document provides comprehensive guidance for banks on effective model risk management, emphasizing that rigorous model validation, sound development, implementation and use, governance and control mechanisms, board and senior management oversight, policies and procedures, controls and compliance, and an appropriate incentive and organizational structure are all vital elements.

## Overview of Model Risk Management

1. For the purposes of this document, a model is a quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process input data into quantitative estimates through information input, processing, and reporting components.
2. **Model risk occurs primarily because** a model may have fundamental errors and produce inaccurate outputs against its design objective and intended business uses, **or because** even a fundamentally sound model may be used incorrectly or inappropriately outside the environment, limitations, or assumptions for which it was designed.
3. A **guiding principle** for managing model risk is effective challenge of models, meaning critical analysis by objective, informed parties who can identify model limitations and assumptions and produce appropriate changes through incentives, competence, and influence.

## Model Development, Implementation, and Use

### Model Development and Implementation

1. An **effective development process** begins with a clear statement of purpose to ensure that model development is aligned with the intended use, and the design, theory, and logic underlying the model should be well documented and generally supported by published research and sound industry practice.
2. The **data and other information** used to develop a model are of critical importance, so there should be rigorous assessment of data quality and relevance, appropriate documentation, and clear identification, justification, and tracking of data proxies, external data, nonrepresentative information, and adjustment assumptions.
3. **Model testing** evaluates the model's components and overall functioning to determine whether it performs as intended by checking accuracy, robustness, stability, limitations, behavior over input ranges, assumptions, poor-performance situations, extreme values, downstream impacts, and the purpose, design, execution, results, commentary, and documentation of test plans.
4. Banks should ensure that **the more judgmental and qualitative aspects** of their models are sound, and that any judgmental or qualitative adjustments to statistical output are conducted in an appropriate and systematic manner and are well documented.

### Model Use

1. **Model use provides additional opportunity** to test whether a model is functioning effectively and to assess performance over time, while model users can provide valuable business insight and constructive challenge but their input should be evaluated carefully because user challenges may be weak, non-comprehensive, or asymmetric.
2. **Reports** used for business decision making play a critical role in model risk management and should be clear and comprehensible, especially when they provide a range of estimates under different input-value scenarios and assumption values to indicate accuracy, robustness, stability, and limitations.
3. Because all models are imperfect representations of reality, an understanding of **model uncertainty and inaccuracy**, whether quantified through potential impacts or confidence intervals or assessed qualitatively, is an important outcome of effective model development, implementation, and use.
4. **Conservative use of models** can be prudent through explicit adjustments to inputs, calculations, outputs, reliance, or supplemental analysis, but banks should justify and substantiate claims of conservatism and avoid using conservatism as an impediment to proper model improvement or application.

## Model Validation

1. Validation involves **a degree of independence from model development and use** because it should generally be done by people who are not responsible for development or use and do not have a stake in whether a model is determined to be valid.
2. Staff doing validation should have the **requisite knowledge, skills, and expertise**, including technical expertise and familiarity with the line of business using the model and the model's intended use.
3. Staff conducting validation work should have **explicit authority to challenge** developers and users and to elevate their findings, including issues and deficiencies, so that they are appropriately addressed in a timely and substantive manner.
4. The **range and rigor of validation activities** conducted prior to first use of a model should be in line with the potential risk presented by use of the model, and significant deficiencies should prevent use, restrict use under tight constraints, or require rejection if too severe.
5. Validation activities should continue **on an ongoing basis** after a model goes into use to track known limitations, identify new ones, and ensure that changes in markets, products, exposures, activities, clients, or business practices do not create new model limitations.
6. Banks should conduct a **periodic review**, at least annually but more frequently if warranted, of each model to determine whether it is working as intended and whether existing validation activities are sufficient.

### Three Key Elements of Comprehensive Validation

1. **Evaluation of conceptual soundness** assesses the quality of model design and construction by reviewing documentation, empirical evidence, methods, selected variables, limitations, assumptions, judgment, developmental evidence, data relevance, sensitivity analysis, and stress testing.
2. **Ongoing monitoring** confirms that the model is appropriately implemented and is being used and performing as intended through process verification, regular testing, evaluation of limitations, code and data controls, report review, analysis of overrides, and benchmarking against alternative internal or external data or models.
3. **Outcomes analysis** compares model outputs to corresponding actual outcomes through appropriate quantitative and qualitative tests, including back-testing, to evaluate model performance, investigate observed variation, and require adjustment, recalibration, or redevelopment when performance is poor.

### Validation of Vendor and Other Third-Party Products

1. Banks should ensure **appropriate processes for selecting vendor models** by requiring developmental evidence explaining product components, design, intended use, testing results, limitations, assumptions, problematic uses, ongoing performance monitoring, outcomes analysis, and modifications or updates over time.
2. Banks are expected to **validate their own use of vendor products** by relying where necessary on sensitivity analysis and benchmarking, documenting and justifying customization choices, assessing vendor data and assumptions for relevance, and conducting ongoing monitoring and outcomes analysis using the bank's own outcomes.

## Governance Policies, and Controls

### Board of Directors and Senior Management

1. The board of directors provides model risk governance at the **highest level** by establishing a bank-wide approach and ensuring that a strong model risk management framework fits into the broader risk management of the organization and reflects tolerance for model risk.
2. Senior management generally **executes and maintains the model risk management framework** by establishing policies and procedures, ensuring compliance, assigning competent staff, overseeing development and implementation, evaluating results, ensuring effective challenge, reviewing validation and audit findings, taking remedial action, and reporting significant model risk to the board.

### Policies and Procedures

1. Banks should **formalize model risk management activities with policies and procedures** that are consistent with this guidance and commensurate with the bank's complexity, business activities, corporate culture, and organizational structure.
2. Policies should emphasize testing and analysis, promote targets for model accuracy, standards for acceptable discrepancies, procedures for review and response to unacceptable discrepancies, and processes used to select and retain vendor models.
3. Policies should **address the prioritization, scope, and frequency of validation activities**, including standards for validation before production, ongoing validation, validation of vendor models and third-party products, and detailed documentation of the model risk management framework.
4. Policies should **identify roles and assign responsibilities** within the model risk management framework with clear detail on staff expertise, authority, reporting lines, continuity, use of external resources, and integration into the overall framework.

### Roles and Responsibilities

1. Ownership means **business units and model owners are ultimately accountable** for model risk associated with business strategies and for ensuring that models are properly developed, implemented, used, validated, approved, identified when new or changed, and supported with information for validation.
2. Control means model **risk taken by business units should be governed through** risk measurement, limits, monitoring, independent validation and review, effective challenge, communication of issues, corrective action plans, and authority to restrict model use or grant controlled temporary exceptions.
3. Compliance means model owners and risk-control staff **must comply with policies** through specific processes, documentation, and tracking of model development, implementation, use, and validation activities so that compliance with policy is transparent.

### Internal Audit

1. Internal audit's role is to a**ssess the overall effectiveness of the model risk management framework**, including its ability to address both types of model risk for individual models and in the aggregate, rather than to duplicate model risk management activities.
2. Internal audit should **verify** that acceptable policies are in place and that model owners and control groups comply with those policies, including timely validation, appropriate controls for validation weaknesses, accurate and complete inventories, limits on model usage, documented model updates, documentation standards, supporting systems, and reliable data.
3. Internal audit also has an important role in ensuring that validation work is conducted properly and that appropriate effective challenge is carried out by evaluating the objectivity, competence, organizational standing, incentives, and rigor of internal and external validation participants.

### External Resources

1. Although model risk management is an internal process, a bank **may engage external resources to help execute activities** such as model validation and review, compliance functions, or support for internal audit when the added knowledge and additional effective challenge justify the costs and time required.
2. Whenever external resources are used, the bank should **specify the activities** to be conducted in a clearly written and agreed-upon scope of work and designate an internal party to understand, evaluate, verify, track, address, incorporate, coordinate, and plan contingencies for that work.

### Model Inventory

1. Banks should maintain a comprehensive set of information for models implemented for use, under development for implementation, or recently retired, while assigning a specific party to maintain a firm-wide inventory of all models to assist in evaluating aggregate model risk.
2. **General guidelines** for a model inventory include describing purpose, designed products, actual or expected usage, restrictions, inputs and sources, underlying components, outputs and intended use, proper functioning, last updates, policy exceptions, responsible individuals, validation dates, and expected validity time frame.

### Documentation

1. **Detailed documentation** of model development and validation should be sufficient for parties unfamiliar with a model to understand how it operates, its limitations, key assumptions, continuity of operations, policy compliance, recommendations, responses, and exceptions.
2. Banks should therefore **provide incentives to produce effective and complete model documentation**, with model developers responsible for thorough and updated documentation and other participants documenting ongoing monitoring, process verification, benchmarking, outcomes analysis, model selection, validation, and third-party approaches.
3. An **effective validation report** should articulate reviewed model aspects, highlight potential deficiencies across financial and economic conditions, determine whether adjustments or compensating controls are warranted, and include a clear executive summary with model purpose, validation results, major limitations, and key assumptions.

## Conclusion

Banks should confirm that their model risk management practices conform to this guidance for model development, implementation, use, and validation, while maintaining strong governance and controls through policies and procedures commensurate with risk exposures, business activities, and the extent and complexity of model use.

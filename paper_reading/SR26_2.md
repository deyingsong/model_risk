# Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management (SR26-2)

## Definitions

- **model:**
    - A “complex quantitative method, system, or approach that applies statistical, economic, or financial theories to process input data into quantitative estimates.”
    - Includes traditional statistical/quantitative and non-generative, non-agentic AI models.
    - Excludes simple arithmetic, deterministic rule-based processes/software, and generative or agentic AI models.

- **model risk:**
    - “The potential for adverse financial consequences associated with models,” from decisions based on model output.
    - Influenced by inherent risk, exposure, purpose, and use.

    - **inherent risk:**
        - Reflects assumptions, complexity, input quality, and data constraints.
        - Increases with complexity and the criticality/number of assumptions.

    - **model exposure:**
        - Significance of model output to business decisions.
        - Greater for larger portfolios or business impact; often measured quantitatively.

    - **model purpose:**
        - Qualitative consideration of nature and importance of use.
        - Regulatory or financial-risk models are generally higher risk.
        - Together with exposure, determines materiality.

    - **model use:**
        - Depends on understanding limitations.
        - Use beyond intended purpose adds uncertainty and risk.
        - Extensions require analysis of new use, limitations, and controls.

## Guidance on model risk management

- Sets a risk-based approach tailored to model risk profile and size/complexity.
- Practices should be commensurate with risk, materiality, and context.

### Overview

- **Sound practice involves assessing model risk individually and in aggregate.**
    - Aggregate risk reflects model interactions/dependencies, common assumptions/data/methodologies, and simultaneous multi-model effects.

- **Sound model risk management also involves effective challenge.**
    - Critical analysis by objective experts throughout the lifecycle.
    - Requires expertise, independence, and standing/influence to effect change.

- **Size and complexity matter.**
    - Range and rigor depend on size/complexity.
    - Practices may be tailored by model, with rigor commensurate with risk.
    - Model risk should be considered in broader business-risk context.

- **Magnitude of model risk.**
    - Reflects inherent risk in materiality context, determined by exposure and purpose.
    - A sound model can still create high risk if misapplied or misused.

### Model development

- **Alignment with purpose and policy**
    - Development aligns with purpose, business use, and policy.
    - Not purely technical; judgment affects design, inputs, assumptions, methodology, and risk.

- **Clear statement of purpose**
    - Begins with a clear purpose to align with intended use.
    - Supports data, methodology, and testing choices.

- **Input from model users**
    - User input adds business context and practical insight.
    - Managers may question methods/assumptions, especially unexpected outcomes.
    - Constructive engagement improves understanding and quality.

- **Testing during development**
    - Testing evaluates whether the model performs as intended.
    - May include out-of-sample/out-of-time testing, alternatives, and data quality/relevance/input assessment.
    - Rigor is commensurate with complexity and materiality.

### Model use

- Effective use depends on understanding limitations and ongoing performance assessment.
- Use beyond intended purpose adds uncertainty/risk; new use requires analysis of usage, limitations, controls.

### Model validation


- **What validation provides**
    - Reliability insight based on assumptions, methods, data, and relevant theories.
    - Characterizes risk and reveals performance deterioration.
    - Meaningful deviations may warrant adjustment/recalibration/redevelopment.

- **Timing, nature, and frequency**
    - Vary by purpose, methodology, changes, data limitations, and constraints.
    - Generally occurs before first use.
    - Urgent pre-validation use requires attention to limitations, stakeholders, and controls.

- **Quality of validation**
    - Depends on rigor/effectiveness, not merely structure.
    - Requires technical knowledge and modeling expertise.

- **Residual model risk**
    - Material risk can remain.
    - Users should communicate limitations, monitor performance, review relevance, and supplement output.

- **Components of model validation**

    - **conceptual soundness**
        - Assesses/documents design, choices, assumptions, judgments, data selection, construction, and testing.
        - Theoretical construction, interpretability, or benchmarking may apply.

    - **outcomes analysis**
        - Compares outputs with real-world outcomes relative to objectives and business use.
        - May include development testing, monitoring reports, back-testing, outlier analysis.
        - Quantitative analysis evaluates expert judgment.

### Model monitoring

- Evaluates whether a model performs as expected as products, exposures, activities, clients, data, or markets change.
- Deterioration may warrant overlays/adjustment/redevelopment.
- Frequency and scope depend on nature, new data/approaches, and materiality.

### Governance and control

- **General governance principle**
    - Lifecycle components benefit from policies and controls.
    - Policies define expectations and a framework for risk-based practices.
    - Procedures establish monitoring/control and resources.
    - Sophistication reflects model usage and size/complexity.

- **roles and responsibilities**
    - Clear roles, responsibilities, and accountability support MRM.
    - Responsibilities span the lifecycle.
    - Internal audit evaluates rigor, effectiveness, and policy implementation, not duplicating development/validation.
    - External resources require oversight and integration.

- **model inventory**
    - Comprehensive inventory is common practice.
    - Effective inventory supports individual and aggregate risk management.
    - Detail may vary by complexity and model usage.

- **documentation**
    - Supports risk management and continuity.
    - Tracks recommendations, responses, exceptions, remediation.

### Third-party products

- Model risk management principles remain applicable.
- Vendor products should be validated internally or externally.

## Comparison between SR11-7 and SR26-2

| Dimension | SR 11-7  | SR 26-2 |
|---|---|---|
| Overall tone | Comprehensive supervisory guidance with detailed expectations for development, implementation, use, validation, governance, policies, controls, documentation, and audit. | Principle-based guidance emphasizing that model risk management should vary by each banking organization’s model risk profile, size, complexity, and business risks. |
| Supervisory force | Says banks should ensure internal policies and procedures are consistent with the guidance’s risk management principles and supervisory expectations. | Explicitly says it does not set enforceable standards or prescriptive requirements, and non-compliance alone will not result in supervisory criticism, though unsafe or unsound practices can still lead to action. |
| Scope of covered institutions | Applies broadly to OCC- and Federal Reserve-supervised banks, with application scaled to risk exposures, business activities, and model complexity. | Expected to be most relevant to banking organizations with over $30 billion in total assets, while smaller organizations are generally excluded unless they have significant model-risk exposure. |
| Agencies involved | Issued by the Federal Reserve and OCC. | Issued by the OCC, Federal Reserve, and FDIC, making the interagency framing broader. |
| Definition of “model” | Defines model broadly as a quantitative method, system, or approach applying statistical, economic, financial, or mathematical theories, techniques, and assumptions to process input data into quantitative estimates. | Narrows the definition to a complex quantitative method, system, or approach applying statistical, economic, or financial theories, and explicitly excludes simple arithmetic spreadsheet calculations and deterministic rule-based processes without such theories. |
| AI treatment | Does not discuss generative AI or agentic AI. | Explicitly says generative AI and agentic AI models are not within the scope because they are novel and rapidly evolving, while traditional statistical / quantitative and non-generative, non-agentic AI models remain covered. |
| Model-risk framing | Explains model risk mainly through two causes: fundamental model errors and incorrect or inappropriate model use. | Reframes model risk through inherent risk, exposure, purpose, use, and materiality, with model materiality determined by exposure and purpose. |
| Materiality / proportionality | Discusses materiality, saying more extensive and rigorous frameworks are needed when models materially affect business decisions or failure would be harmful. | Makes proportionality central: model risk management should be commensurate with model materiality, organization complexity, and broader business risks. |
| Model development | Gives detailed expectations around theory, methodology, documentation, data quality, proxies, testing, information systems, and judgmental adjustments. | Keeps the same core ideas but condenses them: purpose, user input, data/methodology/testing, and rigor commensurate with model complexity and materiality. |
| Model use | Separates model use into user challenge, business reports, uncertainty/inaccuracy, and conservative use of models. | Focuses more compactly on understanding limitations, ongoing performance assessment, user feedback, and risks of extending a model beyond intended purpose. |
| Validation | Provides a detailed validation framework: independence, competence, authority, pre-use validation, ongoing validation, annual periodic review, and three key elements. | Combines validation and monitoring into one section, and makes validation rigor depend on model approach, use, and materiality; it also allows that urgent business needs may require use before validation is complete, with compensating controls. |
| Three validation elements | Presents three core elements: conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring including process verification and benchmarking, and outcomes analysis including back-testing. | Retains the same broad components, but makes them less rigid: conceptual soundness, outcomes analysis, and ongoing model monitoring, with examples such as interpretability, benchmarking, back-testing, outlier analysis, overlays, adjustment, or redevelopment. |
| Governance and controls | Has detailed subsections on board/senior management, policies and procedures, roles and responsibilities, internal audit, external resources, model inventory, and documentation. | Condenses governance into broad principles: clear policies, effective controls, clear roles, accountability, conflict-of-interest awareness, internal audit if used, external resource oversight, model inventory, and documentation. |
| Board / senior management detail | Gives detailed duties: board ultimate responsibility, senior management execution, policies, staffing, challenge, validation and audit findings, remedial action, and reporting to the board. | Does not separately elaborate board and senior management responsibilities in the same detailed way; it folds governance into lifecycle accountability and policy/control expectations. |
| Internal audit | Provides detailed internal audit expectations, including assessing overall framework effectiveness, verifying policies, checking model inventory, limits, documentation, data reliability, and validation challenge. | States more generally that internal audit, where part of MRM practices, should not duplicate model development or validation but should evaluate whether practices are rigorous, effective, and implemented according to policy. |
| Vendor / third-party models | Treats vendor models inside the validation section and gives detailed expectations on vendor selection, developmental evidence, vendor testing, assumptions, customization, benchmarking, own-outcome monitoring, and contingency plans. | Moves vendor and third-party products to a standalone final section, emphasizing that proprietary constraints may limit access to code/data/methodology, but banks should still understand conceptual soundness, design, development data, performance, monitoring, outcomes analysis, and customization. |
| Documentation | Very detailed: documentation should allow unfamiliar parties to understand operation, limitations, assumptions; banks should incentivize documentation; validation reports should include executive summaries, purpose, results, limitations, and assumptions. | Much shorter: adequate documentation supports continuity, tracking recommendations/responses/exceptions, and managing remediation efforts. |

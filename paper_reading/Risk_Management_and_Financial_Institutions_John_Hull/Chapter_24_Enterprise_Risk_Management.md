- Enterprise risk management (ERM) is the holistic, big-picture approach to risk management in which the risk management function **identifies potential adverse events and their full consequences** rather than evaluating market risk, credit risk, operational risk, and other risks only separately.
- COSO's definition makes the points that ERM should involve the board, be a component of company strategy, identify potential adverse events, define and manage risks within risk appetite, and help the company achieve its objectives as a core part of strategic planning and execution.

## Risk appetite

- One dimension of a financial institution's risk appetite is the loss it is prepared to incur if a worst-case scenario happens, which can be expressed as an enterprise-wide **value at risk (VaR)** or **expected shortfall (ES)** measure with a specified time horizon and confidence level.
- $\beta = \frac{R - R_F}{R_M - R_F + N^{-1}(1 - p)\sigma_M}$, where $\beta$ is the portfolio beta, $R$ is the return threshold that has probability $p$ of being exceeded in one year, $R_F$ is the risk-free return, $R_M$ is the expected market return, $\sigma_M$ is the standard deviation of the market return, and $N^{-1}(1-p)$ is the inverse cumulative normal value, so the formula determines the beta consistent with a particular VaR objective and implies investing the beta fraction in the market and the remainder $(1-\beta)$ in the risk-free asset.
- The Bowman paradox is the **empirical finding of a negative relationship between risk and return** for companies in most sectors, with further evidence that more troubled firms often take less justifiable risks.
- example: Wells Fargo's aggressive cross-selling strategy put branch employees under unrealistic sales pressure, leading them to order credit cards and create checking and savings accounts without clients' consent, damaging the bank's reputation, employee relationships, and customer trust.
- Risk concentration is an important element of risk appetite because the risk appetite statement should **limit exposures** to any one company, sector of the economy, market variable, or similar source of concentrated loss.

## Risk culture

- A financial institution's risk culture is all about **how decisions are made**, and a good risk culture means decisions are made in a disciplined way with careful consideration of possible outcomes, risks, rewards, and the organization's risk appetite.
- Decisions often involve **trade-offs between short-term profits and medium- to long-term risks**, such as when an immediate profit from selling a product may later create reputational damage, lawsuits, or other serious problems.
- example: The product sold by Bankers Trust to Procter & Gamble had a fairly high probability of saving 75 basis points on funding and a small probability of costing a huge amount of money, illustrating how a poor risk culture focuses on short-term profit and ignores longer-term repercussions.
- example: In the Santander-Metro do Porto rail deal, MdP saved 3% on funding for the first two years but accepted the risk that later funding costs could become extremely high because the quarterly spread was cumulative and rose rapidly when three-month Euribor moved outside the 2% to 6% range.
- example: The ABACUS deal involved Goldman Sachs creating a synthetic ABS CDO and, it was alleged, selling it to a client without fully explaining that John Paulson's hedge fund had helped choose mortgage exposures likely to incur huge losses, producing short-term profit but long-term reputational damage and a $550 million SEC fine.

## Identifying major risks

- **History** can provide guidelines for identifying major risks, for example by asking how the financial institution would fare if a recession as severe as the 2007-2009 crisis occurred again.
- The impact of **current and emerging trends** should be carefully analyzed because changes such as tighter bank regulation, higher capital and liquidity requirements, restrictions on proprietary trading, and competition from less regulated shadow banks can alter a financial institution's key exposures.

### Cognitive biases

- **Cognitive biases** are tendencies for human beings to think in certain ways and be less than perfectly rational, and they can impair the effective identification of risks.
- **Anchoring** occurs when people evaluating a potential outcome attach too much importance to the first estimate made, adjust only slightly from it, and fail to consider the full range of possible outcomes, especially adverse ones.
- **Availability** is the bias in which recent information is given undue weight, so risk managers may be ignored after a run of good experience and become more influential only after a crisis.
- **Representativeness** occurs when an individual categorizes a situation based on a pattern of previous experiences or beliefs, which can support quick decisions but also create close-mindedness and stereotyping when past experience is not representative of future scenarios.
- **Inverting the conditionality** is the probability mistake of treating $\mathrm{Prob}(D|TP)$ as equal to $\mathrm{Prob}(TP|D)$, even though a 99% accurate disease test with a disease rate of 1 in 10,000 implies that the chance of actually having the disease after a positive result is only about 1%.
- The challenge for ERM is **identifying tail risks and estimating the probabilities of the adverse scenarios giving rise to them as well as possible**, while recognizing that cognitive biases and overreliance on normal distributions can lead these risks to be underestimated.

### Strategic risk management

- Risk is **a function of how poorly a strategy will perform if the ‘wrong’ scenario occurs**: Michael Porter's statement means strategic risk management should examine what assumptions a strategy depends on and how badly it will perform if competitors, customers, or adverse external events create the wrong scenario.
- After a particular risk has been identified, the **alternative courses of action** are to avoid it by exiting the business or investment, reduce its probability or impact, adjust plans to reduce risks, share or insure part of the risk, or accept it by taking no action.

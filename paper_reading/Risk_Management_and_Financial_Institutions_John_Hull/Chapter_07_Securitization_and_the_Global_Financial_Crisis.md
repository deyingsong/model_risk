# Chapter 7 Securitization and the Global Financial Crisis

## Summary

This chapter is a case study in how model assumptions, incentives, and opacity can jointly create systemic failure. The mortgage crisis was not caused only by bad loans; it was amplified by securitization structures, negotiated ratings, re-securitization, weak underwriting, misaligned compensation, and investor overreliance on AAA labels. ABS and ABS CDO waterfalls transformed mortgage cash flows into tranches whose losses were highly sensitive to house-price declines, default clustering, and falling recovery rates. Models that assumed benign correlations or treated structured BBB tranches like ordinary BBB bonds understated tail losses.

The daily takeaway is to challenge any model whose output looks attractive because risk has been sliced, transferred, or hidden. Validate collateral quality, underwriting standards, incentive alignment, tranche waterfall logic, correlation assumptions, recovery-rate dependence, and liquidity under stress. Ratings should be treated as inputs to investigate, not substitutes for analysis. Stress testing should use severe macro scenarios, higher default correlation, lower recovery rates, and market illiquidity, especially for structured or re-securitized products. Governance should flag regulatory arbitrage, short-term profit incentives, opaque data, and products that generate unusually high returns for apparently low capital.

## The U.S. Housing Market

- The **S&P/Case-Shiller composite-10 index** tracks house prices for ten major metropolitan areas in the United States and shows that, in about the year 2000, house prices started to rise much faster than in the previous decade, with low interest rates from 2002 to 2005 contributing but mortgage lending practices largely fueling the bubble.

### The relaxation of lending standards

- **Adjustable rate mortgages (ARMs)** were developed with a low teaser rate lasting two or three years followed by a rate liable to be much higher, and a “2/28” ARM, for example, has a fixed rate for two years and then floats for the remaining 28 years.
- The government was not regulating mortgage lenders because since the 1990s **the U.S. government had been trying to expand home ownership** and had been applying pressure to mortgage lenders to increase loans to low- and moderate-income households, while courts decided that national standards should prevail over state attempts to curtail predatory lending.
- **“Liar loans”** describe mortgages where applicants sometimes lied on application forms knowing that no checks would be carried out, and **“NINJA” (no income, no job, no assets)** refers to borrowers with no income, no job, and no assets.

### The buble bursts

- In the second half of 2006, **house prices started to edge down** because demand declined as prices increased and some borrowers with teaser rates could no longer afford their mortgages when the teaser rates ended, leading to foreclosures, more houses for sale, negative equity, more defaults, and a self-feeding decline in house prices.
- Because mortgages are non-recourse in some states, the borrower has a **free American-style put option** and can at any time sell the house to the lender for the principal outstanding on the mortgage, making default optimal when negative equity exists.
- **As foreclosures increased, mortgage losses also increased** because houses in foreclosure were often surrounded by other houses for sale, were sometimes in poor condition, and imposed legal and other fees on banks, causing recovery rates in some areas in 2008 and 2009 to fall as low as 25%.

## Securitization

- **Securitization** is the process by which originators of mortgages sell portfolios of mortgages to companies that create products for investors from them, thereby transferring risk in financial markets and supporting the originate-to-distribute model.

### Asset-backed securities (ABS)

- An **asset-backed security (ABS)** is a security created from the cash flows of financial assets such as loans, bonds, credit card receivables, mortgages, auto loans, and aircraft leases, with assets sold to a special purpose vehicle and cash flows allocated to tranches.
- There are two ways of looking at an ABS: with reference to the waterfall, cash flows go first to the senior tranche, then to the mezzanine tranche, and then to the equity tranche; in terms of losses, principal losses are first borne by the equity tranche, then by the mezzanine tranche, and then by the senior tranche.
- The ABS is designed so that the senior tranche is rated AAA, the mezzanine tranche is typically rated BBB, and the equity tranche is typically unrated, with ratings treated as “negotiated ratings” because the creator seeks to make the senior tranche as large as possible without losing its AAA credit rating.

### ABS CDOs

- An **ABS CDO**, or Mezz ABS CDO, is an ABS created from the mezzanine tranches of ABSs that were themselves created from subprime mortgages, and its senior tranche was often rated AAA.
- **Many banks lost money investing in the senior tranches of ABS CDOs** because these AAA-rated investments promised returns above funding costs and required minimal capital, but losses on underlying mortgage portfolios could quickly wipe out junior ABS CDO tranches and then impose large losses on the senior tranche.

### CDOs and ABS CDOs in practice

- **The risks in the AAA-rated tranches of ABSs and ABS CDOs were higher than either investors or rating agencies realized**  because their models assumed default correlations that were too low for stressed housing-market conditions and incorrectly treated BBB-rated ABS tranches as equivalent in risk to BBB-rated bonds despite their very different loss distributions.

## The Losses

- **The defaults on mortgages in the United States had a number of consequences:** The defaults on mortgages in the United States caused losses for financial institutions and other investors holding ABS and ABS CDO tranches, losses and lawsuits for some mortgage originators, a flight to quality, sharply higher credit spreads, difficulty for non-financial companies in obtaining bank loans, and sharply higher interbank lending rates.
- In the second half of 2007, rating agencies downgraded tranches of ABSs and ABS CDOs, their markets became very illiquid, and investors realized that they did not understand the tranches as well as they had thought and had relied too much on credit ratings.
- **Banks are now paying a price for the GFC**  because they are required to keep more capital than before, maintain certain liquidity ratios, and face increased oversight and activity restrictions under legislation such as Dodd–Frank.

## What went wrong?

- **'Irrational exuberance'**  describes the behavior of mortgage lenders, investors in ABS and ABS CDO tranches, and protection sellers who assumed that U.S. house prices would continue to increase or at least not decrease, and who did not consider the possibility of a widespread decline.
- **Factors contributed to the crisis that started in 2007**  included lax lending standards, products that transferred mortgage credit risk to investors, rating agencies entering structured products with little historical data, complex products with inaccurate or incomplete information about underlying assets, investors relying on ratings rather than their own analysis, and AAA-rated structured products offering high returns relative to AAA bonds.

### Regulatory arbitrage

- **Regulatory arbitrage** refers to banks securitizing mortgages and buying the securitized tranches because the regulatory capital required for tranches in the trading book was less than the regulatory capital required for the mortgages themselves in the banking book.

### Incentives

- **Agency costs** describe situations where incentives make the interests of two parties in a business relationship not perfectly aligned, and the process by which mortgages were originated, securitized, and sold to investors was riddled with such costs.
-  Two key incentive problems were that mortgage originators, property valuers, ABS and ABS CDO creators, and rating agencies were rewarded for **producing and rating profitable securitized products rather than ensuring long-term credit quality**, while financial-institution employees receiving end-of-year bonuses were encouraged to **focus on short-term profits** even if severe losses followed later.


## Lessons from the Global Financial Crisis

- The eight lessons are that 
    - risk managers should watch for irrational exuberance; 
    - use stressed rather than normal-market correlations; 
    - recognize that recovery rates decline when default rates increase; 
    - ensure that compensation incentives encourage decisions in the organization’s interests; 
    - treat deals that seem too good to be true as requiring further analysis; 
    - avoid relying only on ratings; 
    - value transparency because opaque markets can dry up after negative news; 
    - and recognize that re-securitization such as ABS CDOs and CDOs of CDOs was a badly flawed idea.
